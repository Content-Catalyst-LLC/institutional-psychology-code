# Validation Plan

A real institutional norms study should validate claims across several layers.

## 1. Construct validity

Confirm that indicators actually measure intended concepts.

Examples:

- Does "normative stability" measure coordination or suppressive conformity?
- Does "social enforcement" distinguish constructive correction from punishment of dissent?
- Does "expectation convergence" include affected groups?
- Does "legitimacy alignment" vary by group and institutional history?
- Does "norm change readiness" include sanction cost?

## 2. Behavioral validation

Compare reported expectations with actual behavior:

- compliance
- dissent
- nonparticipation
- whistleblowing
- complaint patterns
- meeting participation
- service uptake
- exit or avoidance
- informal enforcement
- retaliation risk

## 3. Institutional validation

Assess whether policies, training, incentives, role expectations, reporting systems, complaint systems, and leadership behavior reinforce or contradict stated norms.

## 4. Historical validation

Ask what histories shape present norms. Review public records, community memory, prior harms, lawsuits, audits, reports, organizational histories, and reform promises.

## 5. Sensitivity analysis

Test whether conclusions change when:

- expectation convergence is lower
- legitimacy alignment is lower
- social enforcement is suppressive
- unequal normative burden is higher
- fragmentation pressure is higher
- sanction cost is higher
- learning capacity is lower
- alternative norm visibility is higher
- norm stability is disaggregated by group

## 6. Distributional validation

Check whether aggregate norm measures hide unequal burden, exclusion, code-switching, credibility gaps, administrative burden, professional gatekeeping, or historical harm.

## 7. Participatory validation

Invite affected communities, frontline staff, low-resource actors, monitored actors, domain experts, public-interest advocates, and marginalized groups to review interpretation.
