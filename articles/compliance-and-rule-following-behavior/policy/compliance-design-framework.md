# Compliance Design Framework

## Compliance quality criteria

A rule-following system should be evaluated across multiple criteria:

1. **Rule clarity**  
   Are obligations, exceptions, and procedures understandable?

2. **Behavioral feasibility**  
   Can actors comply under real-world constraints?

3. **Legitimacy**  
   Are rules and authorities perceived as fair, justified, and respectful?

4. **Communication quality**  
   Are rules communicated clearly, consistently, accessibly, and in time?

5. **Norm support**  
   Do social, professional, or organizational expectations support substantive compliance?

6. **Enforcement credibility**  
   Are consequences consistent, proportional, and not selective?

7. **Burden justice**  
   Are learning, documentation, psychological, technological, and appeal costs visible and fair?

8. **Contestability**  
   Can people ask questions, correct mistakes, appeal decisions, and challenge rules?

9. **Substantive alignment**  
   Does compliance advance the underlying purpose of the rule?

10. **Adaptive learning**  
   Do compliance breakdowns lead to rule revision, communication improvement, and burden reduction?

## Outcome typology

| Outcome | Description |
|---|---|
| Formal compliance | Rule text or procedures are followed |
| Substantive compliance | Behavior aligns with rule purpose |
| Strategic compliance | Visible requirements are met while purpose is avoided |
| Defensive compliance | Documentation protects actors from blame |
| Coerced compliance | Rule-following occurs primarily because alternatives are unavailable |
| Fragile compliance | Apparent compliance exists with weak legitimacy |
| High-burden compliance | Compliance exists but costs are unfairly distributed |
| Legitimate compliance | Rule-following is clear, fair, feasible, trusted, contestable, and purpose-linked |
