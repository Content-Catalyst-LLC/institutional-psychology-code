# Research Design: Compliance and Rule-Following Behavior

## Conceptual framing

This scaffold treats compliance as a behaviorally mediated institutional relationship rather than a simple obedience outcome. Rules, procedures, enforcement, audits, standards, and expectations matter, but rule-following depends on how actors interpret authority, experience legitimacy, understand rules, evaluate fairness, perceive burden, trust others, respond to norms, and anticipate consequences.

The scaffold distinguishes:

1. **Formal compliance**  
   Adherence to rule text, procedures, forms, deadlines, and documented requirements.

2. **Substantive compliance**  
   Alignment with the underlying institutional, public, professional, civic, or safety purpose of the rule.

3. **Strategic compliance**  
   Visible adherence that preserves opportunities for evasion, loophole use, metric gaming, or goal distortion.

4. **Defensive compliance**  
   Documentation and procedural behavior focused on avoiding blame rather than improving institutional responsibility.

5. **Fragile compliance**  
   High apparent compliance paired with low legitimacy, weak trust, norm erosion, selective rule application, high burden, or visible hypocrisy.

6. **High-burden compliance**  
   Compliance that appears strong while imposing disproportionate learning, documentation, technological, psychological, or appeal costs.

7. **Justice-sensitive compliance**  
   Rule-following systems that distinguish willful violation from incapacity, ambiguity, exclusion, unjust rules, or justified distrust.

## Core hypotheses

### H1: Legitimacy strengthens voluntary compliance.

Actors are more likely to follow rules when institutions and procedures are perceived as fair, rightful, respectful, and consistent.

### H2: Cognitive clarity strengthens compliance, especially under high burden.

Clear communication and interpretable rules reduce predictable errors, avoidance, over-compliance, and administrative failure.

### H3: Norm support reinforces rule-following when trust is strong.

People are more likely to comply when they believe others are also following rules and when peer expectations support adherence.

### H4: Enforcement works better when legitimacy is high.

Sanctions and monitoring can support compliance, but they are more durable when perceived as fair, proportional, and not selective.

### H5: Compliance burden weakens legitimate rule-following.

High learning, documentation, technology, financial, psychological, and appeal costs can convert compliance into exclusion.

### H6: Apparent compliance can hide fragility.

Forms, audits, training completions, and low violation rates can conceal strategic compliance, defensive documentation, coercion, or norm erosion.

## Research design options

- synthetic cross-sectional modeling
- dynamic rule-following simulation
- enforcement-legitimacy interaction analysis
- clarity-burden interaction analysis
- norm-trust interaction analysis
- compliance burden audit
- selective rule-application review
- fragile compliance diagnostics
- high-burden compliance review
- public administration case study
- organizational compliance case study
- platform governance case study
- professional ethics case study
- qualitative process tracing

## Responsible interpretation

The model does not determine whether any real person, group, agency, firm, community, worker, student, or institution is compliant, noncompliant, legitimate, or deviant. It structures reasoning about rule-following, legitimacy, burden, clarity, norms, enforcement, trust, and institutional design. Real compliance analysis requires legal context, qualitative inquiry, historical evidence, public participation, burden audits, affected-community voice, and justice-sensitive interpretation.
