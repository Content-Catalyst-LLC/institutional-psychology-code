# Method Notes

## Why model compliance behavior?

Compliance is often treated as obedience to rules, but institutional psychology shows that rule-following is shaped by legitimacy, fairness, norms, trust, communication, cognitive clarity, enforcement credibility, administrative burden, power, and institutional memory.

A useful model can separate:

- legitimacy
- fairness
- incentive alignment
- norm support
- enforcement credibility
- communication quality
- cognitive clarity
- trust
- adaptive learning
- compliance burden
- selective rule application
- defensive compliance
- hypocrisy visibility
- norm failure

## Why synthetic data?

Synthetic data allows transparent demonstration without using confidential records, vulnerable communities, employees, students, patients, regulated entities, citizens, or sensitive administrative information.

## Why include fragile compliance?

A system can look compliant while becoming fragile underneath. High completion rates, clean audit results, and low violation records can coexist with low trust, high burden, strategic compliance, or suppressed reporting.

## Why include burden and selective rule application?

Compliance distributes work, risk, surveillance, proof, and consequence. Aggregate compliance may hide unequal documentation burden, appeal burden, language burden, technological burden, or monitoring exposure.

## What this scaffold cannot do

It cannot classify real people or organizations as compliant or noncompliant. It cannot justify surveillance, coercion, or punitive rule systems. It cannot replace legal analysis, public participation, historical context, qualitative research, or domain expertise.
