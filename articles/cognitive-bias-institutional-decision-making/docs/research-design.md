# Research Design: Cognitive Bias in Institutional Decision-Making

## Conceptual framing

This scaffold treats cognitive bias as a dynamic interaction between human cognition and institutional structure. Institutions do not simply inherit biased individuals. They can stabilize bias through dashboards, reporting channels, committee procedures, expert cultures, risk frameworks, historical memory, performance metrics, budget baselines, and governance routines.

The scaffold distinguishes:

1. **Individual bias pressure**  
   Overconfidence, anchoring, confirmation pressure, availability effects, loss aversion, status quo preference, and base-rate neglect.

2. **Group and hierarchy bias**  
   Conformity pressure, authority gradients, consensus norms, shared-information bias, professional-frame bias, and groupthink.

3. **Information-system bias**  
   Filtering distortion, metric tunnel vision, dashboard compression, distorted escalation, and false clarity.

4. **Memory and path-dependence bias**  
   Prior assumptions, precedent, past success narratives, inherited categories, and institutional memory.

5. **Power-protective bias**  
   Reputational defensiveness, legitimacy protection, narrative control, and interpretation that shields authority from correction.

6. **Corrective capacity**  
   Structured dissent, red-team review, premortems, independent audit, feedback openness, psychological safety, and decision traceability.

7. **Justice-sensitive correction**  
   Affected-community evidence, burden audits, dissenting memory, marginalized voice, and contestability of official interpretations.

## Core hypotheses

### H1: Bias pressure lowers decision quality when corrective capacity is weak.

Institutions may possess evidence but still make poor decisions if bias pressure overwhelms review, dissent, and feedback.

### H2: Structured dissent is especially valuable under high conformity pressure.

Dissent mechanisms matter most when hierarchy, status, and consensus norms suppress challenge.

### H3: Feedback openness is especially valuable when evidence is negative or disconfirming.

Institutions learn better when bad news, complaints, near misses, and weak signals can revise assumptions.

### H4: Metric tunnel vision increases bias when justice-sensitive voice is weak.

Metrics can support learning, but they can also hide lived harm, exclusion, burden, and qualitative evidence.

### H5: Power protection sustains bias by making some interpretations costly.

Bias persists when acknowledging evidence would threaten reputation, authority, legal exposure, budgets, or dominant narratives.

### H6: Apparent procedural rationality can hide fragile judgment.

Institutions may follow formal process while dissent is weak, assumptions are untested, and evidence is filtered.

## Research design options

- synthetic cross-sectional modeling
- dynamic decision-quality simulation
- structured-dissent and conformity interaction analysis
- feedback-openness and filtering-distortion interaction analysis
- justice-sensitive voice and metric tunnel-vision interaction analysis
- high-bias environment review
- fragile judgment diagnostics
- debiasing sensitivity analysis
- decision-record review
- forecast calibration study
- metric audit
- red-team and premortem process audit
- public governance case study
- organizational strategy case study
- platform governance case study
- regulatory decision case study
- healthcare safety case study
- environmental governance case study
- qualitative process tracing

## Responsible interpretation

The model does not determine whether any real organization, agency, community, worker, student, firm, or institution is biased, rational, competent, or flawed. It structures reasoning about institutional bias pressure, corrective capacity, information quality, voice, power, memory, learning, and decision quality. Real institutional bias analysis requires legal context, qualitative inquiry, historical evidence, participatory review, burden audits, affected-community voice, and justice-sensitive interpretation.
