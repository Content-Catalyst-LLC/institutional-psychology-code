# Debiasing Design Framework

## Debiasing quality criteria

An institutional debiasing system should be evaluated across multiple criteria:

1. **Assumption visibility**  
   Are decision assumptions explicitly documented and reviewable?

2. **Information quality**  
   Are evidence sources accurate, contextualized, diverse, and timely?

3. **Structured dissent**  
   Are red teams, premortems, alternate scenarios, and dissent records meaningful?

4. **Corrective review**  
   Do audits and retrospectives challenge reasoning, not merely outcomes?

5. **Feedback openness**  
   Can negative evidence, complaints, near misses, and weak signals revise decisions?

6. **Psychological safety**  
   Can people surface uncertainty and disagreement without retaliation?

7. **Metric accountability**  
   Are dashboards and proxies audited for distortion and exclusion?

8. **Justice-sensitive voice**  
   Are affected-community evidence, burden audits, and dissenting memory included?

9. **Power accountability**  
   Are reputation, authority, and legitimacy pressures made visible?

10. **Decision traceability**  
   Can people see how evidence shaped or failed to shape decisions?

## Outcome typology

| Outcome | Description |
|---|---|
| Disciplined judgment | Evidence is challenged, assumptions are visible, and correction is consequential |
| Procedural confidence | Process exists but does not create real epistemic challenge |
| Fragile judgment | Decision quality appears high while dissent is weak and distortion is high |
| High-bias environment | Bias pressure is high while correction and feedback openness are weak |
| Symbolic debiasing | Training or review language appears without changing decision architecture |
| Justice-centered correction | Affected voice, burden evidence, and contested interpretations shape revision |
