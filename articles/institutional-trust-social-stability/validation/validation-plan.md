# Validation Plan

A real institutional trust study should validate claims across several layers.

## 1. Construct validity

Confirm that indicators actually measure intended concepts.

Examples:

- Does "trust" measure earned confidence or surface compliance?
- Does "fairness" include distributional and procedural fairness?
- Does "accountability" measure correction, not only complaint intake?
- Does "recognition" measure influence, not just participation?
- Does "repair capacity" include material change?

## 2. Behavioral validation

Compare reported trust with actual behavior:

- participation
- complaint use
- appeal use
- service uptake
- voluntary compliance
- defensive compliance
- exit or avoidance
- disclosure and candor
- willingness to cooperate under uncertainty

## 3. Institutional validation

Assess whether procedures, accountability systems, repair mechanisms, data systems, appeals, and burden-reduction pathways are documented and accessible.

## 4. Historical validation

Ask what institutional histories shape present trust expectations. Review public records, community memory, prior harms, lawsuits, audits, reports, and reform promises.

## 5. Sensitivity analysis

Test whether conclusions change when:

- fairness is lower
- accountability is lower
- repair capacity is lower
- administrative burden is higher
- visible violation pressure is higher
- arbitrariness pressure is higher
- trust is disaggregated by group
- legitimacy is measured separately from trust

## 6. Distributional validation

Check whether aggregate trust metrics hide unequal treatment, administrative burden, access barriers, exclusion, surveillance, or historical harm.

## 7. Participatory validation

Invite affected communities, frontline staff, low-resource actors, monitored actors, domain experts, public-interest advocates, and marginalized groups to review interpretation.
