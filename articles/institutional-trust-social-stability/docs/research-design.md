# Research Design: Institutional Trust and Social Stability

## Conceptual framing

This scaffold treats institutional trust as an earned expectation generated through repeated institutional conduct. Trust is not merely attitude, branding, or sentiment. It is a behavioral infrastructure that allows people to cooperate, comply, disclose, wait, participate, and coordinate when continuous verification is impossible.

The scaffold distinguishes:

1. **Trust foundations**  
   Consistency, competence, fairness, transparency, accountability, integrity, recognition, and repair capacity.

2. **Trust pressures**  
   Arbitrariness, visible violation, hypocrisy, administrative burden, fragmentation, and historical memory of institutional harm.

3. **Behavioral consequences**  
   Voluntary compliance, cooperation, participation, candor, tolerance for uncertainty, and reduced verification burden.

4. **Stability dynamics**  
   Trust, legitimacy, voluntary compliance, cooperation, and repair capacity can reinforce social stability.

5. **Trust breakdown**  
   Visible violations, unequal treatment, opaque decision-making, administrative burden, and uncorrected harm can produce defensive compliance or distrust.

6. **Justice-sensitive trust analysis**  
   Trust is unevenly distributed because institutional histories are unequal. Distrust can be rational, protective, and evidence-based.

7. **Repair and memory**  
   Trust restoration depends on acknowledgment, correction, accountability, restitution, and institutional memory, not messaging alone.

## Core hypotheses

### H1: Trust rises when fairness, accountability, competence, and repair capacity are jointly strong.

Trust depends on repeated evidence that institutions can perform, treat people fairly, and correct failure.

### H2: Visible violations damage trust faster than routine reassurance rebuilds it.

Trust is asymmetric. Institutional betrayal may outweigh many ordinary successes.

### H3: Repair capacity moderates the relationship between failure and trust collapse.

Institutions with credible repair can absorb failure better than institutions that deny or minimize harm.

### H4: Administrative burden reduces trust, especially among groups already exposed to unequal treatment.

Proof burdens, waiting, complexity, stigma, and opaque processes communicate distrust from the institution toward the public.

### H5: Trust supports social stability through voluntary compliance and cooperation.

High-trust systems require less coercion and can govern conflict more effectively.

### H6: Aggregate trust scores can hide unequal trust.

An institution can be trusted on average while remaining deeply untrustworthy to marginalized or historically harmed communities.

## Research design options

- synthetic cross-sectional modeling
- dynamic trust simulation
- fairness and accountability interaction analysis
- recognition and repair interaction analysis
- trust and social-stability modeling
- administrative burden diagnostics
- fragile trust-environment review
- high-distrust pressure review
- trust-repair sensitivity analysis
- trust-breakdown case study
- public-service trust review
- platform-governance trust review
- legal-institution trust review
- healthcare trust review
- environmental governance trust review
- qualitative process tracing

## Responsible interpretation

The model does not determine whether real people, communities, agencies, firms, or institutions are trustworthy or untrustworthy. It structures reasoning about trust foundations, trust breakdown, legitimacy, repair, institutional memory, unequal burden, and social stability. Real trust analysis requires legal context, qualitative inquiry, historical evidence, participatory review, affected-community voice, burden audits, and justice-sensitive interpretation.
