# Validation Plan

A real collective action study should validate claims across several layers.

## 1. Construct validity

Confirm that indicators actually measure intended concepts.

Examples:

- Does "trust" distinguish trust in other actors from trust in institutions?
- Does "cooperation" measure substantive contribution rather than symbolic participation?
- Does "free-riding" distinguish opportunism from incapacity, exclusion, or dissent?

## 2. Behavioral validation

Compare institutional expectations with actual behavior:

- participation rates
- contribution records
- compliance behavior
- resource-use patterns
- free-riding indicators
- enforcement records
- burden distribution

## 3. Institutional validation

Assess whether rules, incentives, monitoring, enforcement, communication, and accountability systems are documented.

## 4. Process-tracing validation

Ask whether the proposed sequence is plausible:

```text
institutional design → trust and legitimacy → cooperation → collective outcome → trust update
```

## 5. Sensitivity analysis

Test whether conclusions change when:

- trust is lower
- legitimacy is lower
- free-riding pressure increases
- burden inequality increases
- hypocrisy becomes visible
- communication quality changes
- enforcement becomes more or less credible
- fairness is included or excluded

## 6. Distributional validation

Check whether aggregate cooperation hides unequal contribution burdens, risk, hidden labor, monitoring, or benefit distribution.

## 7. Participatory validation

Invite affected communities, frontline staff, low-resource actors, marginalized groups, domain experts, and public-interest stakeholders to review interpretation.
