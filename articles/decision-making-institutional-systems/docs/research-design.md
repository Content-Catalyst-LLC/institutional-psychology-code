# Research Design: Decision-Making in Institutional Systems

## Conceptual framing

This scaffold treats institutional decision-making as a distributed process rather than a single act of rational choice. Institutions decide through roles, routines, committees, hierarchies, memory systems, dashboards, reporting channels, incentives, legal frameworks, professional cultures, technical systems, and affected-community relationships.

The scaffold distinguishes:

1. **Bounded rationality**  
   Cognitive, informational, temporal, and procedural limits on decision-making.

2. **Decision architecture**  
   Organizational structure, decision rights, escalation rules, review steps, and authority distribution.

3. **Incentive alignment**  
   Whether rewarded behavior matches long-run institutional purpose.

4. **Information flow**  
   Whether relevant signals reach decision authority with enough context to remain meaningful.

5. **Legitimacy and power**  
   Who can define the problem, interpret evidence, control timelines, and authorize action.

6. **Corrective capacity**  
   Dissent, review, premortems, red teams, audit, and feedback mechanisms.

7. **Justice-sensitive decision quality**  
   Whether affected people can shape problem definition, interpretation, burden assessment, and contestation.

8. **Memory and learning**  
   Whether assumptions, dissent, outcomes, and lessons are preserved and used to revise future decisions.

## Core hypotheses

### H1: Decision quality rises when information flow and corrective capacity are jointly strong.

Information alone is not enough. Signals must reach processes capable of challenge and revision.

### H2: Legitimacy improves decision durability when affected people can influence the decision frame.

Consultation without influence may create symbolic participation rather than institutional trust.

### H3: Bounded-rationality pressure reduces decision quality when decision architecture lacks review and memory.

Complexity becomes dangerous when institutions compress uncertainty without preserving assumptions.

### H4: Bias distortion and power protection reduce feedback openness.

Institutions may protect reputation and authority by reframing negative evidence as anomaly or communication failure.

### H5: Metric fixation weakens justice-sensitive decision quality.

Dashboards and target systems can hide burdens, lived experience, and distributional effects.

### H6: Fragile decision environments can appear high-performing.

Institutions may produce formally strong decisions while information flow and corrective capacity remain weak.

## Responsible interpretation

The model does not determine whether any real organization, agency, community, worker, student, firm, or institution makes good or bad decisions. It structures reasoning about institutional decision quality, evidence, incentives, legitimacy, power, uncertainty, correction, memory, and voice. Real decision analysis requires legal context, qualitative inquiry, historical evidence, participatory review, burden audits, affected-community voice, and justice-sensitive interpretation.
