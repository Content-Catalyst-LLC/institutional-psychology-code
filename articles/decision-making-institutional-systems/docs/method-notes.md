# Method Notes

## Why model decision quality institutionally?

Institutional decisions are produced through distributed cognition, layered authority, information systems, incentives, legitimacy claims, memory, and feedback. Decision quality cannot be reduced to individual intelligence or data availability. It depends on whether the institution can convert distributed knowledge into accountable judgment under uncertainty.

A useful model can separate:

- bounded-rationality pressure
- organizational structure quality
- incentive alignment
- information-flow effectiveness
- legitimacy
- uncertainty management
- corrective capacity
- justice-sensitive voice
- memory quality
- feedback openness
- bias distortion
- power protection
- metric fixation
- siloing
- premature closure

## Why synthetic data?

Synthetic data allows transparent demonstration without using confidential records, vulnerable communities, employees, students, patients, regulated entities, citizens, or sensitive administrative information.

## Why include justice-sensitive voice?

Decision quality is incomplete if it ignores who bears the burden, whose knowledge counts, and whether affected people can contest the frame of decision.

## Why include power protection?

Institutions may protect authority, reputation, legal standing, or budget narratives by resisting evidence that requires revision. Decision-quality analysis must make these pressures visible.

## What this scaffold cannot do

It cannot classify real people or institutions as competent or incompetent. It cannot justify surveillance, coercion, or punitive governance. It cannot replace legal analysis, public participation, historical context, qualitative research, or domain expertise.
