# Decision Architecture Framework

## Decision-quality criteria

An institutional decision system should be evaluated across multiple criteria:

1. **Problem definition**  
   Is the decision problem explicit, contestable, and broad enough to include affected realities?

2. **Evidence quality**  
   Are evidence sources relevant, diverse, timely, contextualized, and uncertainty-aware?

3. **Information flow**  
   Can signals travel across levels and roles without losing meaning?

4. **Incentive alignment**  
   Does the system reward behavior that matches long-run institutional purpose?

5. **Corrective capacity**  
   Are dissent, review, premortems, red teams, audits, and revision triggers meaningful?

6. **Legitimacy**  
   Is the process transparent, intelligible, fair, and contestable?

7. **Uncertainty management**  
   Are scenarios, sensitivity tests, monitoring triggers, and staged commitments used?

8. **Justice-sensitive voice**  
   Are affected-community evidence, burden audits, and marginalized voice included early enough to shape decisions?

9. **Memory and feedback**  
   Are assumptions, dissent, outcomes, and lessons preserved for future revision?

10. **Power accountability**  
   Are reputation, authority, legal exposure, and budget-protective pressures made visible?

## Outcome typology

| Outcome | Description |
|---|---|
| Accountable judgment | Evidence, correction, legitimacy, memory, and voice are connected to decision authority |
| Procedural confidence | Process exists but does not create real challenge or revision |
| Fragile decision environment | Apparent quality exists while information flow and correction are weak |
| High-distortion environment | Bias and power protection dominate while feedback openness is weak |
| Symbolic review | Review language appears without changing assumptions or authority |
| Justice-centered decision-making | Affected voice, burden evidence, and contestability shape the decision frame |
