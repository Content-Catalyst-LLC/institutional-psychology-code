# Enforcement Design Framework

## Enforcement quality criteria

An institutional enforcement system should be evaluated across multiple criteria:

1. **Rule clarity**  
   Are expectations, obligations, and consequences understandable?

2. **Monitoring quality**  
   Can the system see relevant behavior, risk, and outcomes?

3. **Information quality**  
   Is enforcement evidence reliable, complete, auditable, and interpretable?

4. **Sanction credibility**  
   Are consequences consistent, proportional, meaningful, and not arbitrary?

5. **Legitimacy**  
   Are enforcement processes perceived as fair, justified, and mission-linked?

6. **Incentive alignment**  
   Does enforcement support substantive institutional purpose rather than gaming?

7. **Accountability reach**  
   Does enforcement reach actors with real decision authority?

8. **Burden justice**  
   Are compliance costs and reporting burdens necessary, proportionate, and fairly distributed?

9. **Learning capacity**  
   Do enforcement findings lead to correction, rule revision, and institutional memory?

10. **Contestability**  
   Can affected actors challenge rules, classifications, sanctions, and enforcement decisions?

## Outcome typology

| Outcome | Description |
|---|---|
| Formal enforcement | Enforcement mechanisms exist but behavioral effect is uncertain |
| Compliance theater | Visible compliance exists without substantive responsibility |
| Defensive compliance | Actors document to avoid blame rather than correct risk |
| Selective enforcement | Monitoring and sanctions fall unevenly |
| High-burden enforcement | Enforcement appears effective but compliance costs are unequal |
| Fragile enforcement | Apparent effectiveness exists with weak legitimacy |
| Legitimate enforcement | Monitoring, information, sanctions, learning, fairness, and accountability reach align |
