# Method Notes

## Why model social norms?

Social norms are informal institutions that shape cooperation, compliance, trust, legitimacy, and institutional continuity. They can reduce enforcement costs, support mutual predictability, and make rules behaviorally real.

A useful model can separate:

- descriptive norm perception
- injunctive norm perception
- trust
- legitimacy
- sanction intensity
- transmission strength
- institutional reinforcement
- norm conflict
- hypocrisy visibility
- unequal enforcement
- performative compliance
- distributional attention

## Why synthetic data?

Synthetic data allows transparent demonstration without using confidential records, vulnerable communities, employees, students, patients, or sensitive institutional information.

## Why include fragile norm environments?

A norm system may appear strong because compliance is high, while trust is low and cooperation is driven by fear, conformity, unequal sanction, or performative display.

## Why include unequal enforcement?

Norms are often enforced informally and unevenly. Groups with less power may face stronger scrutiny, reputational risk, or compliance burden than dominant groups.

## What this scaffold cannot do

It cannot determine which norms are legitimate in real institutions. It cannot classify dissent as deviance. It cannot substitute for community voice, legal analysis, history, qualitative research, or public accountability.
