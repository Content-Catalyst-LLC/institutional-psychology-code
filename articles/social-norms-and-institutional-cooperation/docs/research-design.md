# Research Design: Social Norms and Institutional Cooperation

## Conceptual framing

This research scaffold treats social norms as informal institutional systems that shape cooperation by defining what people believe others do, what people believe others approve, how deviation is sanctioned, and whether formal rules are interpreted as legitimate.

The scaffold distinguishes:

1. **Descriptive norm perception**  
   Beliefs about what others actually do.

2. **Injunctive norm perception**  
   Beliefs about what others approve, disapprove, reward, condemn, or expect.

3. **Norm-based cooperation**  
   Cooperation supported by shared expectations, reciprocity, trust, legitimacy, and informal sanctions.

4. **Fragile norm environments**  
   High apparent compliance resting on low trust, fear, performative conformity, or unequal informal sanction.

5. **High-burden norm environments**  
   Norm systems in which some actors carry disproportionate compliance, emotional, cultural, reputational, or social costs.

6. **Norm repair**  
   Institutional effort to clarify, revise, democratize, or legitimate norms when they erode, conflict, or become unjust.

## Core hypotheses

### H1: Descriptive and injunctive norms increase cooperation.

People are more likely to comply when they believe others comply and when they believe others approve of compliance.

### H2: Trust amplifies norm-based cooperation.

Norms support cooperation more effectively when actors believe others will reciprocate and that compliance will not be exploited.

### H3: Legitimacy affects whether sanctions stabilize or corrode cooperation.

Social sanctions can reinforce cooperation when perceived as fair, but can weaken legitimacy when applied unequally.

### H4: Institutional reinforcement stabilizes norms.

Norms are more durable when leadership behavior, routines, training, incentives, and accountability systems reinforce them.

### H5: Norm conflict reduces cooperation.

Competing norms create ambiguity about what behavior is expected and who has authority to define appropriateness.

### H6: High apparent compliance can be fragile.

Outward norm conformity may hide low trust, performative compliance, fear, silence, or unequal enforcement.

## Research design options

- synthetic cross-sectional modeling
- dynamic norm formation and erosion simulation
- descriptive/injunctive norm comparison
- sanction-legitimacy interaction analysis
- trust threshold analysis
- unequal enforcement diagnostics
- norm-conflict sensitivity analysis
- organizational culture analysis
- professional ethics case study
- digital platform norm analysis
- public policy norm intervention review
- qualitative norm mapping

## Responsible interpretation

The model does not determine which real norm should be enforced or treated as legitimate. It structures reasoning about expectation, trust, legitimacy, sanction, transmission, and burden. Real institutional analysis requires legal context, qualitative inquiry, historical evidence, public participation, and justice-sensitive interpretation.
