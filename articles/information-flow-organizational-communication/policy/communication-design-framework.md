# Communication Design Framework

## Communication quality criteria

An institutional communication system should be evaluated across multiple criteria:

1. **Signal quality**  
   Are signals timely, relevant, accurate, contextualized, and diverse?

2. **Channel reliability**  
   Can information move across levels, roles, units, platforms, and jurisdictions?

3. **Interpretive integration**  
   Are multiple forms of knowledge synthesized without erasing dissent or context?

4. **Feedback usability**  
   Does feedback reach decisions, memory, learning, and revision pathways?

5. **Openness**  
   Can bad news, weak signals, dissent, near misses, and uncertainty travel safely?

6. **Escalation access**  
   Can relevant information reach actors with authority to act?

7. **Community voice**  
   Are affected-community experiences treated as evidence and connected to decisions?

8. **Digital transparency**  
   Are data provenance, classification rules, dashboard limits, and algorithmic pathways visible?

9. **Overload management**  
   Does the system reduce noise without suppressing uncomfortable information?

10. **Communicative accountability**  
   Can people trace how information changed or failed to change institutional action?

## Outcome typology

| Outcome | Description |
|---|---|
| Usable information flow | Information travels with context and reaches decisions |
| Data-rich ignorance | Data exists but does not improve institutional understanding |
| Fragile communication | Apparent information effectiveness exists with low openness and high distortion |
| High-overload communication | Communication volume creates false clarity and attention burden |
| Symbolic feedback | Voice is collected but decisions remain unchanged |
| Suppressed communication | Bad news is costly and therefore softened or hidden |
| Metric tunnel vision | Measurable proxies replace institutional reality |
| Communicative accountability | Voice, evidence, and feedback are linked to traceable revision |
