# Method Notes

## Why model information flow?

Institutions depend on distributed knowledge. Frontline actors, communities, technical systems, managers, dashboards, archives, auditors, and governance bodies all hold partial information. Communication determines whether this knowledge becomes integrated institutional understanding or remains fragmented, distorted, delayed, suppressed, or unusable.

A useful model can separate:

- signal quality
- communication quality
- interpretive integration
- feedback usability
- memory retention
- openness and psychological safety
- escalation access
- trust
- community voice
- digital transparency
- distortion loss
- overload
- siloing
- suppression pressure
- metric tunnel vision

## Why synthetic data?

Synthetic data allows transparent demonstration without using confidential records, vulnerable communities, employees, students, patients, regulated entities, citizens, or sensitive administrative information.

## Why include justice and community voice?

Institutions often treat internal records and metrics as more legitimate than affected-community knowledge. A communication system can be formally active and still unjust if it extracts testimony without changing decisions.

## Why include overload?

Modern institutions can become data-rich and insight-poor. Dashboards, alerts, meetings, and metrics may produce false clarity while reducing interpretive capacity.

## What this scaffold cannot do

It cannot classify real organizations or communities as communicatively strong or weak. It cannot justify surveillance, coercion, punitive communication systems, or automated monitoring. It cannot replace legal analysis, public participation, historical context, qualitative research, or domain expertise.
