# Research Design: Crisis, Reform, and Institutional Transformation

## Conceptual framing

This research scaffold treats crisis-driven institutional transformation as a dynamic process in which systemic stress, feedback failure, legitimacy loss, reform-window openness, coalition strength, adaptive capacity, and power configuration interact over time.

The scaffold distinguishes:

1. **Crisis**  
   A disruption that overwhelms ordinary corrective capacity.

2. **Reform**  
   A formal or informal change in rules, routines, authority, accountability, incentives, or institutional design.

3. **Transformation**  
   A deeper reconstitution of institutional logic, authority, legitimacy, and behavioral expectations.

4. **Re-stabilization without transformation**  
   A return to institutional continuity without substantial correction of root causes.

5. **Captured reform**  
   A reform process redirected toward insider protection, elite benefit, symbolic legitimacy, or avoidance of deeper accountability.

## Core hypotheses

### H1: Crisis severity alone is insufficient.

Severe crises do not automatically produce reform. Reform is more likely when crisis severity combines with legitimacy failure, reform-window openness, coalition strength, and adaptive capacity.

### H2: Legitimacy failure is a threshold variable.

Institutions may absorb stress for long periods, but once perceived legitimacy drops below a threshold, compliance, coordination, and public trust can deteriorate rapidly.

### H3: Coalition strength shapes whether crisis becomes reform.

A crisis without organized reform capacity may produce paralysis, repression, symbolic reform, or institutional drift.

### H4: Capture risk weakens transformation quality.

High reform probability does not mean just or meaningful reform. Reform windows can be captured by actors with disproportionate power.

### H5: Distributional attention improves reform legitimacy.

Reforms that attend to unequal harm, marginalized communities, and public accountability are more likely to produce durable legitimacy.

## Research design options

- synthetic cross-sectional modeling
- dynamic simulation
- panel-data extension
- comparative case studies
- historical institutional analysis
- qualitative process tracing
- network analysis of reform coalitions
- mixed-method legitimacy assessment
- public inquiry and after-action report analysis

## Responsible interpretation

The model does not predict real-world reform outcomes. It helps structure reasoning about crisis, reform, legitimacy, and transformation. Real institutional analysis requires historical evidence, legal context, qualitative inquiry, participatory review, and justice-sensitive interpretation.
