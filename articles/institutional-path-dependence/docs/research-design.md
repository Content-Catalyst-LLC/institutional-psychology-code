# Research Design: Institutional Path Dependence

## Conceptual framing

This research scaffold treats institutional path dependence as a dynamic process in which early decisions become self-reinforcing through feedback, legitimacy, coordination, learning, switching costs, and complementarity with other institutions.

The scaffold distinguishes:

1. **Initial conditions**  
   Early rules, resource distributions, political settlements, technical systems, and authority arrangements.

2. **Reinforcement**  
   Repeated behaviors, incentives, expectations, and organizational routines that stabilize the path.

3. **Lock-in**  
   A condition in which deviation becomes increasingly costly, difficult, or illegitimate.

4. **Path-breaking pressure**  
   Crisis, mobilization, legal challenge, technological shift, normative change, or reform capacity that weakens the inherited path.

5. **High-burden lock-in**  
   A path-dependent system that remains stable while imposing unequal burdens on marginalized or less protected groups.

## Core hypotheses

### H1: Path dependence emerges from multiple reinforcing mechanisms.

Increasing returns alone are rarely sufficient. Lock-in is stronger when increasing returns combine with legitimacy, switching costs, learning effects, coordination, and complementarity.

### H2: Legitimacy amplifies switching costs.

When an inherited arrangement is seen as legitimate, alternatives are not only costly but normatively suspect.

### H3: Reform capacity weakens lock-in only when it is credible.

Disruption pressure may weaken the existing path, but path-breaking change requires viable alternatives and implementation capacity.

### H4: High-burden lock-in can be hidden by aggregate stability.

Institutions can appear stable while imposing repeated burdens on less powerful communities.

### H5: Complementarity makes single-site reform difficult.

Institutions are often locked in because surrounding institutions depend on the same categories, rules, routines, data systems, or funding structures.

## Research design options

- synthetic cross-sectional modeling
- dynamic simulation
- historical sequence analysis
- process tracing
- network analysis of institutional complementarity
- path-breaking scenario analysis
- comparative institutional analysis
- mixed-method legitimacy assessment
- distributional burden review

## Responsible interpretation

The model does not determine whether a real institution should be preserved, reformed, or replaced. It helps structure reasoning about historical constraint, lock-in, and reform possibility. Real institutional analysis requires historical evidence, legal context, qualitative inquiry, participatory review, and justice-sensitive interpretation.
