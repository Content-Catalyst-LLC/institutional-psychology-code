# Method Notes

## Why model path dependence?

Institutional path dependence is often discussed as a historical concept, but modeling helps make the reinforcement mechanisms explicit.

A useful model can separate:

- initial conditions
- feedback strength
- increasing returns
- coordination benefits
- learning effects
- legitimacy
- switching costs
- complementarity
- disruption pressure
- reform capacity
- distributional burden

## Why synthetic data?

Synthetic data allows transparent demonstration without using confidential records, vulnerable communities, real employees, or politically sensitive institutional data.

## Why include distributional burden?

A path-dependent system can remain stable by shifting costs to less powerful groups. A lock-in model that ignores unequal burden may mistake institutional persistence for social success.

## Why include reform capacity?

Disruption alone may not break a path. Path-breaking requires credible alternatives, implementation capacity, coalition strength, legitimacy, and cross-system coordination.

## What this scaffold cannot do

It cannot determine whether a real institution should be preserved, reformed, or replaced. It cannot assign responsibility. It cannot substitute for community voice, legal analysis, archival evidence, public inquiry, or historical research.
