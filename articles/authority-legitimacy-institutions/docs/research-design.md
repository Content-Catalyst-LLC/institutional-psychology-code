# Research Design: Authority and Legitimacy in Institutions

## Conceptual framing

This scaffold treats legitimacy as a behavioral and psychological condition that converts formal power into recognized authority. Institutions do not govern through command alone. They depend on people interpreting authority as sufficiently rightful, intelligible, fair, accountable, and worthy of recognition.

The scaffold distinguishes:

1. **Authority foundations**  
   Formal authority clarity, rule clarity, decision rights, procedural order, and review pathways.

2. **Legitimacy foundations**  
   Procedural legitimacy, outcome legitimacy, trust, fairness, social recognition, accountability, and repair capacity.

3. **Legitimacy pressures**  
   Arbitrariness, visible inconsistency, unequal burden, opacity, coercion, historical harm, and symbolic repair.

4. **Behavioral consequences**  
   Voluntary compliance, strategic compliance, defensive compliance, surface compliance, resistance, and legitimacy-based coordination.

5. **Uneven legitimacy**  
   Authority may be legitimate to some populations and coercive, punitive, or exclusionary to others.

6. **Repair and memory**  
   Legitimacy restoration depends on acknowledgment, correction, accountability, restitution, institutional memory, and changed authority relationships.

## Core hypotheses

### H1: Authority-legitimacy strength rises when procedural legitimacy, trust, rule clarity, accountability, and repair capacity are jointly strong.

Authority is more durable when people can understand, contest, and trust the processes by which power is exercised.

### H2: Voluntary compliance rises when legitimacy is strong and enforcement feels less coercive.

Compliance based on recognized rightfulness is more stable than compliance extracted through threat.

### H3: Arbitrariness pressure weakens legitimacy nonlinearly.

Visible inconsistency or arbitrary treatment can rapidly undermine authority, especially when it confirms prior distrust.

### H4: Repair capacity moderates legitimacy failure after visible institutional harm.

Institutions that acknowledge and correct failure can sustain legitimacy better than institutions that deny or minimize harm.

### H5: Unequal burden fractures legitimacy across groups.

Aggregate legitimacy can hide group-specific illegitimacy where some populations face disproportionate proof, waiting, surveillance, sanction, or exclusion.

### H6: Formal authority clarity is insufficient without social recognition.

An institution may have clear authority on paper while lacking legitimacy among affected people.

## Research design options

- synthetic cross-sectional modeling
- dynamic legitimacy simulation
- procedural legitimacy and trust interaction analysis
- accountability and repair-capacity interaction analysis
- formal authority clarity and rule clarity interaction analysis
- legitimacy and voluntary-compliance modeling
- fragile legitimacy-environment review
- high-arbitrariness environment review
- legitimacy-repair sensitivity analysis
- authority breakdown case study
- reform-legitimacy case study
- legal authority case study
- platform governance authority case study
- regulatory legitimacy case study
- organizational leadership legitimacy case study
- qualitative process tracing

## Responsible interpretation

The model does not determine whether real people, communities, agencies, firms, or institutions are legitimate or illegitimate. It structures reasoning about authority, legitimacy, compliance, procedural justice, power, recognition, repair, unequal burden, and institutional memory. Real legitimacy analysis requires legal context, qualitative inquiry, historical evidence, participatory review, burden audits, affected-community voice, and justice-sensitive interpretation.
