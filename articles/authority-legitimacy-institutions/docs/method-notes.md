# Method Notes

## Why model authority and legitimacy?

Institutions exercise power through rules, procedures, offices, classifications, sanctions, expertise, and decisions. Power becomes authority only when it is recognized as rightful enough to deserve compliance. Legitimacy therefore affects governance cost, voluntary compliance, institutional trust, reform capacity, and social stability.

A useful model can separate:

- formal authority clarity
- procedural legitimacy
- outcome legitimacy
- trust
- rule clarity
- social recognition
- accountability
- repair capacity
- fairness
- shared norm support
- arbitrariness pressure
- visible inconsistency
- unequal burden
- opacity pressure
- enforcement coercion pressure

## Why synthetic data?

Synthetic data allows transparent demonstration without using confidential records, vulnerable communities, employees, students, patients, regulated entities, citizens, or sensitive administrative information.

## Why include justice and historical memory?

Authority is not experienced equally. Institutions may appear legitimate to groups that experience them as protection while appearing coercive to groups that experience them as surveillance, exclusion, burden, or punishment.

## What this scaffold cannot do

It cannot score real legitimacy. It cannot justify obedience. It cannot classify communities as irrationally resistant. It cannot replace legal analysis, public participation, historical context, qualitative research, or domain expertise.
