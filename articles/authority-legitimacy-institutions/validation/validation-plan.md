# Validation Plan

A real authority-legitimacy study should validate claims across several layers.

## 1. Construct validity

Confirm that indicators actually measure intended concepts.

Examples:

- Does "legitimacy" measure earned recognition rather than fear-based obedience?
- Does "authority clarity" measure real decision rights and appeal pathways?
- Does "procedural legitimacy" include respect, access, consistency, and review?
- Does "accountability" measure correction, not only complaint intake?
- Does "repair capacity" include material change?

## 2. Behavioral validation

Compare legitimacy indicators with actual behavior:

- voluntary compliance
- strategic compliance
- defensive compliance
- appeal use
- complaint patterns
- protest or resistance
- candor and disclosure
- service uptake
- exit or avoidance

## 3. Institutional validation

Assess whether authority structures, procedures, accountability systems, appeals, enforcement rules, data systems, and repair mechanisms are documented and accessible.

## 4. Historical validation

Ask what institutional histories shape present legitimacy expectations. Review public records, community memory, prior harms, lawsuits, audits, reports, and reform promises.

## 5. Sensitivity analysis

Test whether conclusions change when:

- procedural legitimacy is lower
- trust is lower
- accountability is lower
- repair capacity is lower
- arbitrariness pressure is higher
- unequal burden is higher
- opacity pressure is higher
- enforcement coercion pressure is higher
- legitimacy is disaggregated by group

## 6. Distributional validation

Check whether aggregate legitimacy metrics hide unequal treatment, administrative burden, access barriers, exclusion, surveillance, or historical harm.

## 7. Participatory validation

Invite affected communities, frontline staff, low-resource actors, monitored actors, domain experts, public-interest advocates, and marginalized groups to review interpretation.
