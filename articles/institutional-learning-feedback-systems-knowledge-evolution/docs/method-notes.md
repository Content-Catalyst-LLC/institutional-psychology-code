# Method Notes

## Why model institutional learning?

Institutional learning determines whether experience changes future action. A system can collect information, publish reports, and run review processes without revising assumptions, incentives, routines, memory, or governance. Modeling helps separate activity from learning.

A useful model can separate:

- feedback quality
- memory retention
- communication openness
- interpretive quality
- decision revisability
- psychological safety
- accountability reach
- disconfirming evidence
- institutional inertia
- signal distortion
- memory decay
- defensive routines
- power protection
- feedback delay

## Why synthetic data?

Synthetic data allows transparent demonstration without using confidential records, vulnerable communities, employees, students, patients, regulated entities, citizens, or sensitive administrative information.

## Why include fragile learning?

Learning may appear strong through reports, reviews, pilots, trainings, and dashboards while communication remains closed, bad news remains risky, and old assumptions remain protected.

## Why include power protection and justice?

Institutions do not learn equally from everyone. Evidence from marginalized communities, frontline workers, service users, disabled people, racialized groups, low-income communities, whistleblowers, and lower-status professionals is often ignored or delayed until harm has repeated.

## What this scaffold cannot do

It cannot classify real organizations or communities as adaptive or learning. It cannot justify surveillance, coercion, or punitive learning-culture systems. It cannot replace legal analysis, public participation, historical context, qualitative research, or domain expertise.
