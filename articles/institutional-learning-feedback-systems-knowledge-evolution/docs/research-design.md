# Research Design: Institutional Learning, Feedback Systems, and Knowledge Evolution

## Conceptual framing

This scaffold treats institutional learning as a distributed system process rather than simple information accumulation. Institutions learn only when feedback becomes interpretable evidence, evidence becomes retained memory, memory becomes revised practice, and revised practice becomes durable governance.

The scaffold distinguishes:

1. **Single-loop learning**  
   Correcting errors within existing rules, routines, metrics, or assumptions.

2. **Double-loop learning**  
   Revising the assumptions, goals, metrics, categories, or governing logics that produced recurring error.

3. **Deutero-learning**  
   Learning how to learn: improving feedback architecture, memory systems, dissent protections, interpretation, and revision capacity.

4. **Symbolic learning**  
   Producing reports, reviews, consultation, training, or statements without altering deeper institutional behavior.

5. **Fragile learning**  
   Apparent adaptation paired with weak communication openness, low psychological safety, signal distortion, or limited decision revisability.

6. **High-inertia learning**  
   Apparent learning while institutional inertia and signal distortion remain elevated.

7. **Justice-sensitive learning**  
   Learning systems that include affected communities, marginalized voices, frontline knowledge, burden audits, and accountability for institutional non-learning.

## Core hypotheses

### H1: Feedback quality strengthens learning when communication openness is high.

Feedback becomes consequential when it can travel across institutional boundaries and reach actors with authority to revise practice.

### H2: Memory retention strengthens adaptation when decision revisability exists.

Stored knowledge matters only when institutions can act on it through revised rules, routines, incentives, and governance.

### H3: Psychological safety is especially important when evidence is disconfirming.

Institutions need protected channels for bad news, dissent, near misses, and evidence that threatens dominant narratives.

### H4: Institutional inertia weakens learning even when information exists.

Established routines, metrics, budgets, narratives, and authority structures can preserve obsolete assumptions.

### H5: Signal distortion produces learning failure before formal evidence disappears.

When bad news is filtered, softened, delayed, or reframed, institutions learn from edited reality.

### H6: Apparent learning can hide fragility.

Institutions may publish reviews, revise language, and update procedures while preserving the incentives and power structures that generated recurring failure.

- environmental governance learning case study
- qualitative process tracing

## Responsible interpretation

The model does not determine whether any real organization, agency, community, worker, student, firm, or institution is learning, failing, adaptive, or brittle. It structures reasoning about feedback, memory, interpretation, communication openness, psychological safety, power, inertia, justice, and revision. Real institutional learning analysis requires legal context, qualitative inquiry, historical evidence, public participation, burden audits, affected-community voice, and justice-sensitive interpretation.
