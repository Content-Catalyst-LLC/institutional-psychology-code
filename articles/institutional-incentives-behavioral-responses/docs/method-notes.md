# Method Notes

## Why model institutional incentives?

Institutional incentives shape behavior through reward, penalty, recognition, visibility, status, access, accountability, and burden. They can produce aligned effort, but they can also create gaming, short-termism, signal distortion, crowding out, risk displacement, mission drift, burden transfer, and compliance theater.

A useful model can separate:

- value alignment
- fairness
- information quality
- legitimacy
- learning support
- accountability
- cognitive bias pressure
- metric substitution
- reporting distortion
- behavioral burden
- short-termism
- status inequality
- motivation crowding

## Why synthetic data?

Synthetic data allows transparent demonstration without using confidential records, vulnerable communities, employees, students, patients, regulated entities, citizens, or sensitive administrative information.

## Why include fragile incentive systems?

A system can appear successful because targets are met, rankings rise, and dashboards improve while legitimacy, truthfulness, learning, and mission alignment deteriorate.

## Why include burden and status inequality?

Incentives distribute reward, risk, status, workload, documentation, and pressure. Aggregate performance may hide hidden labor, moral burden, and unequal cost.

## What this scaffold cannot do

It cannot classify real people or organizations as motivated, aligned, high-performing, or low-performing. It cannot justify surveillance, coercion, or punitive performance systems. It cannot replace legal analysis, public participation, historical context, qualitative research, or domain expertise.
