# Research Design: Institutional Incentives and Behavioral Responses

## Conceptual framing

This scaffold treats incentives as behaviorally active institutional architecture rather than simple rewards or penalties. Incentives shape what actors notice, optimize, report, hide, preserve, sacrifice, and learn to value. They can align behavior with institutional purpose, but they can also produce metric substitution, short-termism, defensive reporting, burden transfer, mission drift, and high-performance failure.

The scaffold distinguishes:

1. **Formal incentives**  
   Compensation, promotion criteria, rankings, grants, subsidies, penalties, audits, deadlines, eligibility rules, performance metrics, and sanctions.

2. **Implicit incentives**  
   Prestige, belonging, reputation, status security, informal approval, protection from blame, access, professional identity, and cultural recognition.

3. **Substantive alignment**  
   Incentives that reward behavior aligned with the underlying institutional, public, professional, civic, or safety purpose.

4. **Metric substitution**  
   The replacement of institutional purpose with a narrower proxy, dashboard indicator, ranking, or performance target.

5. **Fragile incentive systems**  
   High apparent performance paired with low legitimacy, weak fairness, reporting distortion, burden transfer, short-termism, or low learning capacity.

6. **High-burden incentive systems**  
   Incentives that appear effective while imposing disproportionate workload, documentation, risk, moral, coordination, or psychological costs.

7. **Justice-sensitive incentive analysis**  
   Evaluation of who defines success, who benefits, who is pressured, who bears the cost, and whose work or harm remains invisible.

## Core hypotheses

### H1: Value alignment strengthens incentive effectiveness.

Incentives are more durable when rewarded behavior is meaningfully connected to institutional purpose rather than narrow proxies.

### H2: Fairness and legitimacy shape behavioral response.

Actors respond more constructively to incentives they perceive as fair, legitimate, and mission-linked.

### H3: Information quality determines whether incentives support learning.

Incentives can support learning only if they preserve truthful information and do not punish bad news.

### H4: Metric substitution weakens institutional purpose.

When measures replace mission, actors can improve measured performance while reducing real value.

### H5: Behavioral burden can hide behind high performance.

Incentive systems may achieve visible goals by shifting cost onto lower-power actors, invisible labor, or future risk.

### H6: Apparent incentive success can hide fragility.

Dashboards, rankings, and targets may improve while trust, truthfulness, learning, and mission alignment deteriorate.

## Research design options

- synthetic cross-sectional modeling
- dynamic incentive simulation
- fairness-legitimacy interaction analysis
- information-learning interaction analysis
- metric-substitution sensitivity analysis
- incentive-burden audit
- reporting-distortion review
- fragile incentive-system diagnostics
- high-burden incentive-system review
- organizational governance case study
- public administration case study
- platform governance case study
- research evaluation case study
- financial incentive case study
- qualitative process tracing

## Responsible interpretation

The model does not determine whether any real person, organization, agency, firm, worker, student, or community is motivated, high-performing, low-performing, aligned, or misaligned. It structures reasoning about incentives, behavior, fairness, information quality, metric substitution, burden, learning, and institutional design. Real incentive analysis requires legal context, qualitative inquiry, historical evidence, public participation, burden audits, affected-community voice, and justice-sensitive interpretation.
