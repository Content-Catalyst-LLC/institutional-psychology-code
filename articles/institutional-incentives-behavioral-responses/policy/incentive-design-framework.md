# Incentive Design Framework

## Incentive quality criteria

An institutional incentive system should be evaluated across multiple criteria:

1. **Value alignment**  
   Does the incentive reward behavior that advances real institutional purpose?

2. **Fairness**  
   Are rewards, burdens, opportunities, and risks distributed fairly?

3. **Legitimacy**  
   Do affected actors regard the incentive and its objective as credible and justified?

4. **Information quality**  
   Does the incentive preserve truthful reporting and early warning?

5. **Learning support**  
   Does the incentive reward correction, adaptation, and honest review?

6. **Metric integrity**  
   Does the metric represent the purpose, or has it become a substitute?

7. **Burden justice**  
   Are workload, documentation, risk, moral, and psychological costs visible and fair?

8. **Time horizon**  
   Does the incentive support long-term resilience rather than only immediate output?

9. **Contestability**  
   Can actors challenge, revise, or appeal distorted incentive effects?

10. **Accountability**  
   Are incentive designers answerable for unintended consequences?

## Outcome typology

| Outcome | Description |
|---|---|
| Aligned incentive | Rewarded behavior advances institutional purpose |
| Proxy incentive | Rewarded behavior improves a measure but not necessarily the mission |
| Gaming | Actors optimize the metric while undermining purpose |
| Short-term incentive | Immediate performance is rewarded while future risk accumulates |
| Burden-shifting incentive | Costs are transferred to less powerful actors |
| Fragile incentive system | Apparent performance exists with weak legitimacy |
| High-burden incentive system | Apparent performance depends on unequal or hidden cost |
| Learning incentive | Incentive supports candor, correction, adaptation, and institutional memory |
