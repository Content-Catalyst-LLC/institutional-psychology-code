# Research Design: Coordination Problems in Institutional Systems

## Conceptual framing

This research scaffold treats coordination as the institutional production of mutual predictability. Institutions coordinate when they help actors converge around shared signals, standards, timing, roles, expectations, and interpretations.

The scaffold distinguishes:

1. **Expectation alignment**  
   Actors form compatible beliefs about what others will do.

2. **Focal-point formation**  
   A rule, standard, signal, deadline, authority, or convention becomes mutually salient.

3. **Operational coordination**  
   Behavior aligns across people, organizations, systems, and time.

4. **Fragile coordination**  
   High observed alignment rests on low trust, weak legitimacy, coercive standardization, or unclear authority.

5. **High-burden coordination**  
   System-wide alignment exists, but some actors carry disproportionate switching, training, compliance, resource, or adaptation costs.

6. **Adaptive coordination**  
   Institutions detect misalignment and revise signals, standards, or procedures without destroying shared expectations.

## Core hypotheses

### H1: Trust increases coordination quality.

Actors align more reliably when they believe others will interpret and follow shared signals predictably.

### H2: Communication clarity reduces miscoordination.

Clear and consistent communication increases alignment, especially when uncertainty is high.

### H3: Focal salience supports equilibrium selection.

Shared focal points help actors converge when multiple possible equilibria exist.

### H4: Authority works best when trusted and legitimate.

Authority signals can coordinate behavior, but contested or illegitimate authority may produce rival signals.

### H5: Competing standards increase coordination fragility.

Multiple plausible standards or focal points fragment adoption and weaken interoperability.

### H6: High alignment can still be unjust.

Coordination may impose unequal adaptation costs or suppress dissent when standards are set without participation.

## Research design options

- synthetic cross-sectional modeling
- dynamic coordination simulation
- equilibrium selection analysis
- trust and focal salience threshold analysis
- standards-adoption analysis
- interoperability review
- authority-signal analysis
- high-burden coordination diagnostics
- communication audit
- after-action review
- network analysis
- process tracing

## Responsible interpretation

The model does not determine which real standard, policy, authority, or equilibrium should prevail. It structures reasoning about alignment, uncertainty, signals, trust, communication, and burden. Real institutional analysis requires legal context, qualitative inquiry, historical evidence, public participation, and justice-sensitive interpretation.
