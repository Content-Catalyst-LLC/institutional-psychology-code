# Method Notes

## Why model institutional coordination?

Coordination problems show that cooperation can fail even when interests are broadly compatible. Actors may want the same outcome but remain uncertain about what others will do, which signal to follow, or which equilibrium will become dominant.

A useful model can separate:

- information quality
- trust
- communication clarity
- focal-point salience
- authority signal strength
- norm strength
- learning capacity
- uncertainty
- competing standards
- adaptation burden
- distributional attention

## Why synthetic data?

Synthetic data allows transparent demonstration without using confidential records, vulnerable communities, real employees, or sensitive administrative data.

## Why include fragile coordination?

A system can appear highly coordinated while resting on low trust, coercive standardization, or a focal point that loses legitimacy under stress.

## Why include high-burden coordination?

Coordination often requires actors to adapt to a chosen standard. Those switching costs may fall unequally on smaller organizations, marginalized communities, frontline staff, or low-resource institutions.

## What this scaffold cannot do

It cannot decide which focal point, standard, policy, or authority should prevail in a real system. It cannot substitute for participation, legal review, historical context, qualitative evidence, or affected-community knowledge.
