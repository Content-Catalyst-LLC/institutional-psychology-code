# Method Notes

## Why model institutional change?

Institutional change is often described as formal reform, but institutions only change deeply when behavior, routines, incentives, trust, legitimacy, and governance practice change as well.

A useful model can separate:

- formal change
- behavioral adaptation
- feedback quality
- governance capacity
- legitimacy
- path dependence
- transition burden
- distributional attention

## Why synthetic data?

Synthetic data allows transparent demonstration without using confidential records, vulnerable communities, real employees, or sensitive administrative data.

## Why include transition burden?

Institutional change can impose real burdens on actors. A system may adapt in aggregate while shifting costs to frontline workers, marginalized groups, or less powerful communities.

## Why include fragile adaptation?

Some institutions appear to adapt successfully but do so with low legitimacy. These adaptations may be unstable, coercive, or likely to trigger later resistance.

## What this scaffold cannot do

It cannot determine whether a real institution should change or how reform should be designed. It cannot assign responsibility. It cannot substitute for community voice, legal analysis, historical evidence, public inquiry, or qualitative research.
