# Research Design: Institutional Change and Behavioral Adaptation

## Conceptual framing

This research scaffold treats institutional change as a recursive process in which formal rules, informal norms, governance structures, organizational routines, and behavioral responses co-evolve over time.

The scaffold distinguishes:

1. **Formal institutional change**  
   Changes in rules, laws, procedures, offices, governance structures, enforcement arrangements, or authority relationships.

2. **Behavioral adaptation**  
   Changes in how individuals, groups, organizations, and publics interpret, comply with, resist, internalize, or work around institutional expectations.

3. **Recursive feedback**  
   The loop through which institutional structures shape behavior, behavior produces outcomes, and outcomes reshape institutions.

4. **Successful adaptation**  
   Change that becomes behaviorally enacted, legitimate, coordinated, and institutionally sustainable.

5. **Fragile adaptation**  
   Change that appears successful on the surface but rests on weak legitimacy, high transition burden, or poor behavioral alignment.

6. **Burden-shifting adaptation**  
   Change that preserves system continuity by transferring costs to less powerful actors or marginalized communities.

## Core hypotheses

### H1: Formal change is insufficient without behavioral adaptation.

Rules can change while routines, expectations, and incentives remain locked into inherited practice.

### H2: Feedback quality matters only when governance capacity can use it.

Institutions may collect information without learning from it.

### H3: Legitimacy increases adaptation durability.

Actors are more likely to adapt when institutional change appears fair, intelligible, accountable, and justified.

### H4: Path dependence dampens adaptation.

Inherited routines, switching costs, and institutional drag reduce the likelihood and depth of change.

### H5: Transition burden can undermine otherwise successful adaptation.

Change can weaken legitimacy when actors experience it as confusing, costly, inequitable, or poorly coordinated.

### H6: Distributional attention improves adaptation quality.

Institutional change is more legitimate when it accounts for who benefits, who bears costs, and whose knowledge is recognized.

## Research design options

- synthetic cross-sectional modeling
- dynamic simulation
- recursive feedback modeling
- legitimacy-threshold analysis
- transition-burden review
- fragile adaptation diagnostics
- process tracing
- organizational case study
- public-policy implementation analysis
- mixed-method legitimacy assessment

## Responsible interpretation

The model does not determine whether a real institution should change or how it should change. It helps structure reasoning about adaptation, legitimacy, governance capacity, and behavioral enactment. Real institutional analysis requires historical evidence, legal context, qualitative inquiry, participatory review, and justice-sensitive interpretation.
