# Validation Plan

A real public goods study should validate claims across several layers.

## 1. Construct validity

Confirm that indicators actually measure intended concepts.

Examples:

- Does "trust" measure trust in other contributors, institutions, or both?
- Does "provision quality" capture access and reliability, not only total output?
- Does "free-riding" distinguish opportunism from inability or exclusion?

## 2. Behavioral validation

Compare formal contribution rules with actual behavior:

- contribution rates
- evasion patterns
- compliance records
- voluntary participation
- public trust
- reported barriers

## 3. Institutional validation

Assess whether governance rules, monitoring systems, enforcement mechanisms, and benefit distribution are documented.

## 4. Process-tracing validation

Ask whether the proposed sequence is plausible:

```text
trust + legitimacy + monitoring + enforcement → contribution → provision quality → updated trust
```

## 5. Sensitivity analysis

Test whether conclusions change when:

- legitimacy is lower
- enforcement is higher
- scale complexity increases
- capture risk increases
- distributional attention is included or excluded

## 6. Distributional validation

Check whether aggregate public goods provision hides unequal access, unequal burden, or unequal monitoring.

## 7. Participatory validation

Invite affected communities, frontline staff, civil society actors, public officials, and domain experts to review interpretation.
