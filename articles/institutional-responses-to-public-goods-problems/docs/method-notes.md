# Method Notes

## Why model public goods institutions?

Public goods problems reveal how individual incentive and collective need can diverge. Modeling helps make visible the role of trust, legitimacy, enforcement, monitoring, and fairness in converting fragile incentives into durable cooperation.

A useful model can separate:

- contribution behavior
- provision quality
- trust
- legitimacy
- enforcement
- monitoring
- scale complexity
- capture risk
- distributional burden

## Why synthetic data?

Synthetic data allows transparent demonstration without using confidential records, vulnerable communities, real employees, or politically sensitive institutional data.

## Why include fragile public goods?

A public good can appear well-provided while resting on low trust, coercive compliance, capture risk, or unequal sacrifice.

## Why include distributional burden?

Public goods provision can reproduce injustice when the benefits are shared unevenly or when some communities carry disproportionate contribution, monitoring, labor, or risk.

## What this scaffold cannot do

It cannot determine how a real public goods system should be governed. It cannot identify moral responsibility. It cannot substitute for community voice, public law, fiscal analysis, ecological evidence, or historical research.
