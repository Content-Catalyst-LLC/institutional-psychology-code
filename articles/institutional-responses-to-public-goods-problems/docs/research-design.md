# Research Design: Institutional Responses to Public Goods Problems

## Conceptual framing

This research scaffold treats public goods provision as a behavioral and institutional problem. Public goods are underprovided when individual incentives diverge from collective need, but durable provision depends on more than coercion. It depends on trust, legitimacy, enforcement, norms, monitoring, coordination, fairness, and scale fit.

The scaffold distinguishes:

1. **Contribution**  
   Individual or organizational input into a shared good.

2. **Provision quality**  
   Whether the public good is actually delivered, maintained, and accessible.

3. **Free-riding opportunity**  
   The perceived ability to benefit without contributing.

4. **Legitimate enforcement**  
   Contribution rules that are not only enforceable but perceived as fair and justified.

5. **Fragile public goods provision**  
   Public goods that appear strong in output terms but rest on weak legitimacy, high capture risk, or unequal burden.

6. **Distributional burden**  
   Unequal contribution, monitoring, punishment, labor, or risk carried by less powerful groups.

## Core hypotheses

### H1: Trust and legitimacy increase contribution.

People contribute more reliably when they believe others are contributing and when governing institutions are perceived as fair, competent, and accountable.

### H2: Enforcement works better when legitimacy is high.

Enforcement can stabilize contribution, but illegitimate enforcement may produce evasion, resentment, or adversarial compliance.

### H3: Monitoring reassures contributors when perceived as fair.

Monitoring reduces free-riding and helps sustain trust, but it can undermine legitimacy if invasive or unequal.

### H4: Scale complexity reduces provision quality.

Large, heterogeneous, anonymous, or transnational systems create more difficult public goods problems.

### H5: High provision can still be fragile.

Public goods provision may appear successful while legitimacy, fairness, or distributional support is weak.

### H6: Distributional attention improves public goods legitimacy.

Institutions are more durable when they address who pays, who benefits, who is monitored, and who has voice.

## Research design options

- synthetic cross-sectional modeling
- dynamic contribution simulation
- free-riding scenario analysis
- trust and legitimacy threshold analysis
- capture-risk sensitivity analysis
- distributional burden review
- polycentric governance case study
- public finance analysis
- commons governance process tracing
- mixed-method legitimacy assessment

## Responsible interpretation

The model does not determine how any real public goods system should be governed. It structures reasoning about contribution, legitimacy, enforcement, and provision quality. Real institutional analysis requires legal context, qualitative inquiry, historical evidence, public participation, and justice-sensitive interpretation.
