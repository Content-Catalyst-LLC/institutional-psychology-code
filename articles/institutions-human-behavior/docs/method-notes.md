# Method Notes

## Why model institutions and behavior?

Institutions become effective only when they are interpreted, enacted, trusted, remembered, and reproduced by human behavior. Formal rules alone do not explain institutional durability. Behavioral alignment depends on legitimacy, norms, incentives, information, memory, trust, role clarity, and learning capacity.

A useful model can separate:

- normative stability
- legitimacy strength
- incentive alignment
- information quality
- memory retention
- learning capacity
- trust reinforcement
- role clarity
- repair capacity
- administrative burden
- opacity pressure
- historical harm pressure
- fragmentation pressure

## Why synthetic data?

Synthetic data allows transparent demonstration without using confidential records, vulnerable communities, employees, students, patients, regulated entities, citizens, or sensitive administrative information.

## Why include justice and historical memory?

Institutions are not experienced equally. What appears stable from one standpoint may appear punitive, extractive, humiliating, or exclusionary from another. Historical memory shapes trust, compliance, refusal, and institutional interpretation.

## What this scaffold cannot do

It cannot score real institutions. It cannot classify real people or communities. It cannot prove legitimacy, trust, institutional health, or justice. It cannot replace legal analysis, public participation, historical context, qualitative research, domain expertise, or affected-community voice.
