# Research Design: Institutions and Human Behavior

## Conceptual framing

This scaffold treats institutions and human behavior as co-constitutive. Institutions shape cognition, incentives, norms, roles, legitimacy, information, memory, and expectations, while behavior reproduces, contests, revises, or destabilizes institutional order over time.

The scaffold distinguishes:

1. **Institutional foundations**  
   Normative stability, legitimacy strength, incentive alignment, information quality, memory retention, learning capacity, trust reinforcement, role clarity, and repair capacity.

2. **Institutional pressures**  
   Fragmentation pressure, opacity pressure, administrative burden, historical harm pressure, distrust, and failed repair.

3. **Behavioral consequences**  
   Behavioral alignment, strategic compliance, voluntary compliance, information withholding, institutional avoidance, participation, dissent, refusal, and reform uptake.

4. **Recursive institutional dynamics**  
   Behavior reinforces institutions through repeated enactment, but disalignment can weaken institutions even when formal structures remain intact.

5. **Justice-sensitive institutional analysis**  
   Institutions may be protective for some groups and punitive or extractive for others. Aggregate behavioral alignment can hide unequal burden, erased memory, or coerced compliance.

6. **Learning and repair**  
   Institutional resilience depends on whether institutions preserve memory, absorb feedback, acknowledge harm, and materially change practice.

## Core hypotheses

### H1: Institutional strength rises when legitimacy, normative stability, trust, information quality, memory retention, and learning capacity are jointly strong.

Institutions become behaviorally real when formal expectations are recognized, interpreted, and enacted as credible.

### H2: Behavioral alignment rises when legitimacy, norms, incentives, role clarity, and trust are strong.

Alignment reflects more than enforcement; it depends on recognition, expectations, and role meaning.

### H3: Fragmentation, opacity, administrative burden, and historical harm weaken institutional strength.

Institutions become fragile when actors no longer share expectations or trust that procedures are intelligible and fair.

### H4: Learning capacity and repair capacity buffer institutional fragility.

Institutions that acknowledge error, preserve memory, and change behavior can retain legitimacy after failure.

### H5: Formal institutional presence is not the same as behavioral reality.

A system can possess formal authority while lacking trust, legitimacy, or expectation alignment.

### H6: Institutional durability is not the same as justice.

Stable institutions can reproduce exclusion, hierarchy, burden, or coercive compliance.

## Research design options

- synthetic cross-sectional modeling
- dynamic institutional-behavior simulation
- legitimacy and trust interaction analysis
- information quality and memory interaction analysis
- learning capacity and fragmentation interaction analysis
- fragile institutional-environment review
- high-fragmentation environment review
- institutional repair and learning sensitivity analysis
- public governance case study
- organizational behavior case study
- legal institution case study
- healthcare trust case study
- platform governance case study
- environmental governance case study
- qualitative process tracing

## Responsible interpretation

The model does not determine whether real institutions are strong, weak, legitimate, illegitimate, trustworthy, or untrustworthy. It structures reasoning about institutions, behavior, legitimacy, trust, information, memory, power, burden, repair, and learning. Real institutional analysis requires legal context, qualitative inquiry, historical evidence, participatory review, burden audits, affected-community voice, and justice-sensitive interpretation.
