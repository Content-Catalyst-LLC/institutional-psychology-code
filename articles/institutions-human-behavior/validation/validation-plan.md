# Validation Plan

A real institutions-and-behavior study should validate claims across several layers.

## 1. Construct validity

Confirm that indicators actually measure intended concepts.

Examples:

- Does "institutional strength" measure behavioral reality rather than coercive stability?
- Does "behavioral alignment" reflect voluntary alignment or fear-based compliance?
- Does "trust reinforcement" vary by group and historical experience?
- Does "memory retention" include histories of harm or only official precedent?
- Does "learning capacity" produce changed behavior?

## 2. Behavioral validation

Compare institutional indicators with actual behavior:

- voluntary compliance
- strategic compliance
- information withholding
- complaint patterns
- appeal use
- participation
- exit or avoidance
- dissent
- refusal
- reform uptake
- bad-news reporting
- candor in meetings

## 3. Institutional validation

Assess whether authority structures, procedures, accountability systems, appeals, reporting systems, data systems, training, incentives, and repair mechanisms are documented and accessible.

## 4. Historical validation

Ask what institutional histories shape present trust and behavior. Review public records, community memory, prior harms, lawsuits, audits, reports, reform promises, and institutional memory systems.

## 5. Sensitivity analysis

Test whether conclusions change when:

- legitimacy strength is lower
- normative stability is lower
- trust reinforcement is lower
- learning capacity is lower
- repair capacity is lower
- fragmentation pressure is higher
- opacity pressure is higher
- administrative burden is higher
- historical harm pressure is higher
- behavioral alignment is disaggregated by group

## 6. Distributional validation

Check whether aggregate institutional-strength metrics hide unequal treatment, administrative burden, exclusion, distrust, surveillance, or historical harm.

## 7. Participatory validation

Invite affected communities, frontline staff, low-resource actors, monitored actors, domain experts, public-interest advocates, and marginalized groups to review interpretation.
