# Research Design: Institutional Memory, Knowledge Retention, and Organizational Continuity

## Conceptual framing

This scaffold treats institutional memory as a dynamic, distributed, and politically shaped systems layer rather than static storage. Institutional memory preserves knowledge across time, but it also shapes interpretation, path dependence, technical continuity, accountability, and future possibility.

The scaffold distinguishes:

1. **Documented memory**  
   Formal records, policies, decision logs, audit trails, archives, metadata, and evidence repositories.

2. **Tacit memory**  
   Experiential knowledge held by practitioners, communities, frontline staff, technical specialists, and long-term institutional actors.

3. **Cultural memory**  
   Shared narratives, myths, norms, identities, reputational judgments, and informal histories.

4. **Process memory**  
   Workflows, templates, procedures, escalation paths, routines, and standard practices that encode past decisions.

5. **Technical memory**  
   Software systems, data schemas, classifications, codebases, dashboards, metadata, and infrastructure dependencies.

6. **Memory justice**  
   Inclusion of affected-community memory, histories of harm, dissenting interpretations, burden records, and public accountability.

7. **Memory fragility**  
   Memory systems that appear strong but depend on key individuals, inaccessible archives, weak metadata, fragmented systems, or selective official narration.

## Core hypotheses

### H1: Documented retention strengthens institutional memory when records remain accessible and contextualized.

Records matter only when they can be found, interpreted, trusted, and connected to present decisions.

### H2: Tacit transfer reduces memory fragility.

Institutions are more resilient when practical knowledge is transferred through mentorship, handoff, succession, and communities of practice.

### H3: Accessibility and interpretive use interact.

Accessible records are most valuable when institutions actually use them in planning, risk review, policy design, and decision-making.

### H4: Revisability reduces harmful path dependence.

Memory supports adaptation when categories, assumptions, routines, and technical systems can be revised responsibly.

### H5: Memory justice changes what institutions are capable of remembering.

Official memory is incomplete when affected-community testimony, histories of harm, and dissenting interpretations are excluded.

### H6: Apparent memory strength can hide fragility.

Large archives, stable routines, and strong traditions may conceal unusable records, mythic memory, selective narration, and path-dependent rigidity.

## Responsible interpretation

The model does not determine whether any real organization, agency, community, worker, student, firm, or institution has good or bad memory. It structures reasoning about memory, continuity, transfer, access, interpretation, revision, power, justice, and fragility. Real institutional memory analysis requires legal context, qualitative inquiry, historical evidence, archival review, public participation, burden audits, affected-community voice, and justice-sensitive interpretation.
