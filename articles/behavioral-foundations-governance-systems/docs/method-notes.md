# Method Notes

## Why model behavioral governance?

Governance systems are not self-executing. They require interpretation, trust, legitimacy, cognitive accessibility, norm support, coordination, enforcement, and learning.

A useful model can separate:

- incentive alignment
- legitimacy
- norm support
- cognitive interpretability
- trust
- coordination quality
- enforcement credibility
- adaptive learning
- perceived fairness
- behavioral burden
- hypocrisy visibility
- power asymmetry

## Why synthetic data?

Synthetic data allows transparent demonstration without using confidential records, vulnerable communities, employees, students, patients, citizens, or sensitive administrative information.

## Why include fragile governance?

A governance system can appear effective while depending on fear, high burden, low trust, unequal monitoring, or performance indicators that conceal lived instability.

## Why include behavioral burden?

Administrative and cognitive burdens distribute access, exclusion, effort, risk, and vulnerability. Burden is part of governance design, not a secondary inconvenience.

## What this scaffold cannot do

It cannot classify real institutions as legitimate or illegitimate. It cannot rank real people, communities, or agencies. It cannot substitute for legal analysis, public participation, historical context, qualitative research, or burden audits.
